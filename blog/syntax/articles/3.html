   Today. we are gonna talk about tag questions. 
        The fundamental principle of tag questions is the tone of the statement contrasts that of the tag question.
        Sounds too perplexing, right?
        Well, what I am capable of is to make difficult stuff super simplified, and my secret weapon is --- examples!!
       Therefore, let's cut to the chase:
  1. They are good learners, aren't they?    Clearly, the statement here is in a positive tone, thus, obviously the tag question shall be in negative tone. So the answer is, therefore, aren't; 
  2. My cat doesn't catch mice, does it?  Here, according to the aforementioned, since the statement is in negative tone, the tone of the tag question for sure ought to be positive.
  3. The example that follows would look a little daunting or bizarre to some rookie learners, but that's fine, as I said before, I am the one to make the hard easy:                            There's little ice in the tea, is there?                    At first glance, this may look pretty straightforward to you, for the statement lacks the common negation word---not, then some may assert that the statement is in positive tone, thus instantly choosing to negative tone. However, if you zoom in and stare a little harder, you would realise that little actually means almost no, which signifies it's in negative tone, so we need to assign a positive tone to the tag question;
  4. The same rule as the above applies to this tag question.      He has few books, does he?                  wherein few means almost not, thus giving the statement a negative tone, which suggests the tag question should be in a positive one.
  5. Now comes the tricky and critical point where we'll discuss a tag question that contains the word-must.                          Some might wonder why the word must is so exceptional. Well, now, I'm going to show you why:                Compare the two tag questions:                  A. She must be a wonderful ballerina, is she?                              B. He must become a great wonderful soccer player,  mustn't he?                If your answer is mustn't for both of the questions, then my response will be: LOL, as it's not so simple and naive a question. How come?                    Let's delve in! The most conspicuous difference between the two questions is the first must is followed by an auxiliary verb BE, whereas in the second just a normal verb.          The first sentence can be interpreted as she is for sure a good ballerina, the we can effortlessly figure out the answer is isn't she;  But, the second question is equivalent to he has to become a wonderful soccer player, then we can, again, easily assume the right answer shall be mustn't he.                                                  This is the end of the chapter with more to follow...
